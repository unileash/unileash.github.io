What is the Great Attractor?

The Great Attractor is a gravitational anomaly in intergalactic space that reveals a region of massive pull. It's located around 150–250 million light-years away in the direction of the Centaurus and Norma constellations. This region appears to be drawing galaxies — including our own Milky Way — toward it at extreme speeds.

The Discovery

In the 1970s and 1980s, astronomers discovered that our Local Group of galaxies, including the Milky Way, is moving at over 600 km/s in a specific direction. This motion couldn't be explained by nearby visible mass alone. The cause of this movement — a mysterious hidden mass — was termed the Great Attractor.

Why is it a Mystery?

The Great Attractor lies behind the Zone of Avoidance, a part of the sky obscured by the dense plane of the Milky Way. This makes it incredibly hard to observe using optical telescopes. Even now, only parts of it can be studied with infrared and X-ray astronomy.

What Might it Be?

Many astronomers believe the Great Attractor is a massive cluster of galaxies, likely part of the even larger Laniakea Supercluster — our galaxy’s “cosmic address.” Others think it may include dark matter, which cannot be seen directly. Some suggest that it could be an even greater hidden structure influencing the motion of thousands of galaxies.

Strange Facts

  • Our Milky Way is moving toward the Great Attractor at over 2.2 million km/h.
  • It’s hidden behind the Milky Way’s dust and gas — making it hard to study.
  • Infrared and X-ray telescopes helped detect galaxy clusters in that region.
  • It may be part of a structure larger than anything we can currently detect.
  • Its gravitational pull affects thousands of galaxies over hundreds of millions of light-years.
