Exoplanets: The Hunt for Other Earths

Exoplanets are planets that orbit stars outside our solar system. Since the discovery of the first exoplanet in the 1990s, astronomers have confirmed thousands of them, with more found every year.

  • The first exoplanet was discovered in 1992 orbiting a pulsar.
  • NASA’s Kepler and TESS missions have revolutionized exoplanet detection.
  • Many exoplanets are located in the "habitable zone" — the region around a star where liquid water could exist.

Some exoplanets are gas giants larger than Jupiter, while others are rocky like Earth. What's even more exciting are Earth-sized planets in habitable zones, which could potentially support life. Scientists categorize them based on size, orbit, temperature, and star type.

Technologies like the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) and upcoming missions are capable of analyzing the atmospheres of exoplanets. This opens the door to detecting biosignatures such as oxygen, methane, and even industrial pollutants.

Why are we so obsessed with exoplanets? Because they may hold the answer to the most important question humanity has ever asked: Are we alone? Every new exoplanet brings us one step closer to answering it.

With thousands already found, and estimates suggesting billions may exist in our galaxy, the age of planetary discovery has just begun. Whether through artificial intelligence, advanced telescopes, or interstellar probes in the future, our search for another Earth continues.
