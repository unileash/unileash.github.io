🪐 Exoplanets

Exoplanets are planets that orbit stars **outside** our solar system. The first confirmed detection came in 1992, and since then, over 5,000 exoplanets have been discovered, many of which could be habitable.

🔭 How Are Exoplanets Found?

Astronomers use several methods to find these distant worlds:

  • Transit Method: Observing dips in a star's brightness as a planet crosses in front of it
  • Radial Velocity: Detecting tiny wobbles in a star's movement due to gravitational pull of orbiting planets
  • Direct Imaging: Rare, but involves capturing actual pictures of exoplanets
  • Gravitational Microlensing: Using the bending of light from a distant star to detect a planet's presence

🌍 Earth-like Worlds

Some exoplanets lie in their star’s habitable zone—the right distance for liquid water. Famous ones include:

  • Kepler-186f: Similar size to Earth, potentially habitable
  • TRAPPIST-1 System: 7 Earth-sized planets orbiting a red dwarf star
  • Proxima Centauri b: Closest known exoplanet, 4.2 light-years away

🧪 What They Tell Us

Studying exoplanets helps us understand:

  • The formation of planetary systems
  • Whether life could exist elsewhere
  • How common Earth-like planets are in the galaxy

🚀 Missions Searching for Life

Space agencies have launched missions like:

  • Kepler: Discovered thousands of exoplanet candidates
  • TESS: Currently scanning the sky for nearby exoplanets
  • James Webb Space Telescope: Can detect atmospheres and look for signs of biosignatures

💭 Could We Ever Visit?

Currently, exoplanets are too far for human travel. The closest one would take over 70,000 years to reach with today’s technology. But concepts like light sails and warp drives are being explored for the future.
