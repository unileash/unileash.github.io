What Are Exoplanets?

Exoplanets, or extrasolar planets, are planets that orbit stars outside our solar system. These worlds vary dramatically — some are giant gas planets like Jupiter, while others may be rocky like Earth or completely alien in composition. The study of exoplanets helps us understand how planets form, how common they are, and whether we are alone in the universe.

The Discovery Era

In 1992, astronomers discovered the first confirmed exoplanets orbiting a pulsar. Then in 1995, the first exoplanet around a Sun-like star — 51 Pegasi b — was found. Since then, over 5,000 exoplanets have been detected, with NASA’s Kepler and TESS missions leading the search.

How Are Exoplanets Found?

Because they are dim and distant, exoplanets are found using indirect methods:

  • Transit Method: Observing a dip in a star’s brightness when a planet passes in front of it.
  • Radial Velocity: Detecting star "wobbles" caused by gravitational pulls from orbiting planets.
  • Direct Imaging: Rare but powerful, using infrared to block starlight and capture planets directly.
  • Gravitational Microlensing: A distant planet’s gravity magnifies light from a background star.

Habitable Zones

The habitable zone is the region around a star where conditions might support liquid water — essential for life as we know it. Earth lies within our Sun’s habitable zone. Finding Earth-like planets in this zone is a key goal of exoplanet exploration. Some exoplanets, like Kepler-442b and TOI-700d, are considered promising candidates.

Bizarre Exoplanets Discovered

  • HD 189733b – Rains molten glass sideways in 7,000 km/h winds.
  • Kepler-16b – A real-life Tatooine that orbits two stars.
  • WASP-12b – So close to its star it’s being torn apart.
  • Gliese 581c – Once thought habitable, now likely a hot greenhouse.
  • 55 Cancri e – Possibly made of diamond.

Fascinating Facts

  • Over 5,000 confirmed exoplanets have been discovered to date.
  • Planets outnumber stars in the galaxy — nearly every star has at least one planet.
  • Some exoplanets orbit pulsars, white dwarfs, and even rogue — starless — paths.
  • Future telescopes may soon analyze their atmospheres for signs of life.
  • The James Webb Space Telescope is already probing exoplanet skies for water, methane, and alien chemistry.
