What is the CMB?

The Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) is the faint afterglow of the Big Bang — the oldest light in the universe. It’s a nearly uniform background radiation filling all of space, created about 380,000 years after the Big Bang when the universe cooled enough for light to travel freely.

Discovery of the CMB

In 1965, Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson accidentally discovered the CMB while working on a radio antenna. They detected a mysterious, persistent noise coming from all directions — and it turned out to be the echo of creation itself. This discovery confirmed the Big Bang theory.

Why is the CMB Important?

The CMB is a snapshot of the early universe. It holds clues to its age, composition, and structure. Tiny fluctuations in temperature across the sky tell scientists how matter was distributed and how galaxies began to form. It is one of the strongest evidences for the Big Bang.

Temperature Fluctuations

Although it appears uniform, the CMB has minute temperature variations — only about 1 part in 100,000. These anisotropies are critical for understanding the large-scale structure of the universe. Satellites like COBE, WMAP, and Planck have mapped these variations in exquisite detail.

The CMB and the Big Bang

The existence of the CMB is strong proof that the universe started from a hot, dense state. As space expanded, this ancient light was stretched into microwave wavelengths. Today, it has cooled to just 2.7 Kelvin — barely above absolute zero — yet it still fills the entire cosmos.

Epic Facts About the CMB

  • The CMB is the farthest thing humans can ever observe.
  • It’s been traveling through the cosmos for over 13.8 billion years.
  • It’s remarkably uniform in all directions — proof of a rapidly expanding early universe.
  • Small temperature differences helped form stars and galaxies.
  • The Planck satellite captured the most detailed CMB map ever in 2013.
