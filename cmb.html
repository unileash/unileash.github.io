The Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB)

The Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) is the afterglow of the Big Bang, a faint microwave radiation that fills the entire universe. It is one of the most crucial pieces of evidence supporting the Big Bang theory.

Discovered in 1965 by Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson, the CMB represents the oldest light we can observeâ€”dating back nearly 13.8 billion years. It is a snapshot of the universe when it was just 380,000 years old, providing an extraordinary glimpse into the early cosmos.

  • The CMB is almost perfectly uniform, with slight temperature fluctuations.
  • These fluctuations are the seeds of galaxy formation.
  • Its uniformity provides strong support for cosmic inflation.

The tiny differences in temperature (just a few microkelvin) tell us about the density variations in the early universe. Over billions of years, gravity pulled denser regions together to form galaxies, stars, and planets.

The CMB is studied by satellites like COBE, WMAP, and Planck. These missions have mapped its temperature variations in exquisite detail, leading to a precise measurement of the age, composition, and curvature of the universe.

Understanding the CMB not only reveals how the universe began but also informs theories of its fate. As we peer deeper into the CMB's secrets, we get closer to answering the fundamental question: Why does the universe look the way it does?
