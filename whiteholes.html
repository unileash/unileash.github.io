What is a White Hole?

A white hole is a theoretical region in spacetime which cannot be entered from the outside, although matter and light can escape from it. It is considered the exact opposite of a black hole. While a black hole pulls everything into it, a white hole explosively pushes everything out.

Theoretical Origins

White holes arise from solutions to the same Einstein field equations that describe black holes. According to mathematical extensions of black hole metrics, a white hole could be what’s on the “other side” of a black hole — suggesting a deep link between the two, possibly through a wormhole.

Are White Holes Real?

As of now, white holes remain purely theoretical. There is no observational evidence for them. Some physicists suggest that the Big Bang itself may be interpreted as a white hole — a sudden, one-time expulsion of all matter in a localized event. Others speculate that black hole evaporation might end in a white hole burst.

Mind-Bending White Hole Facts

  • White holes cannot be entered — only exited.
  • They are solutions to the time-reversal of black hole equations.
  • A white hole might be connected to a black hole via a theoretical Einstein-Rosen bridge.
  • If real, white holes would violate the second law of thermodynamics unless quantum effects are involved.
  • No white holes have ever been detected in nature or in simulations of the cosmos.
  • Some theories claim a dying black hole could “bounce” into a white hole in a final explosion.
