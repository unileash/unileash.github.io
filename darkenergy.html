What is Dark Energy?

Dark energy is one of the greatest mysteries in modern astrophysics. It’s a hypothetical form of energy that permeates all of space and is believed to be responsible for the accelerating expansion of the universe.

Scientists estimate that nearly 68% of the universe is made of dark energy. That’s more than double the amount of dark matter and vastly more than ordinary matter. Yet, we know very little about it.

  • Discovered in 1998 through supernova observations.
  • Challenges Einstein's original theory of gravity.
  • Possibly connected to the cosmological constant he introduced.

Some theories suggest that dark energy might be a new kind of dynamic energy field. Others believe it's simply a property of space. Either way, it causes a repulsive force that counteracts gravity on cosmic scales.

Observations of the cosmic microwave background, baryon acoustic oscillations, and large-scale structure all support the existence of dark energy. It's like a mysterious pressure that pushes galaxies apart faster and faster over time.

If dark energy continues to dominate, the universe may expand forever—ending in a cold, empty state known as the Big Freeze. But if it behaves differently over time, other fates like the Big Rip or cosmic bounce are also possible.

Understanding dark energy isn’t just a science goal—it’s a quest to unlock the destiny of the universe. Missions like ESA’s Euclid and NASA’s Nancy Grace Roman Space Telescope are being designed to probe this enigma with precision.
