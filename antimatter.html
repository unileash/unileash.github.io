Antimatter: The Mirror of Matter

Antimatter is the opposite of ordinary matter. For every particle that exists — like electrons, protons, and neutrons — there's a corresponding antiparticle with the same mass but opposite charge.

  • Antiparticles: Electron → Positron, Proton → Antiproton, Neutron → Antineutron.
  • Annihilation: When matter and antimatter meet, they annihilate each other, releasing pure energy.
  • Creation: Antimatter can be created in high-energy experiments and cosmic ray interactions.

Despite the laws of physics suggesting equal amounts of matter and antimatter should've formed after the Big Bang, our universe is dominated by matter. Why this imbalance exists remains one of science's greatest mysteries — known as **baryon asymmetry**.

Antimatter isn't just theoretical — it has real-world applications. Positrons are used in PET scans for medical imaging. In future tech, antimatter could become a source of immense energy, far beyond what nuclear or chemical fuels can offer.

But there's a big challenge: antimatter is incredibly rare and unstable in our matter-dominated world. Storing it requires magnetic traps in vacuum environments, and producing even a microgram costs billions of dollars.

Understanding antimatter not only helps us explore the fundamental nature of the universe but may also unlock secrets of time, symmetry, and the origin of everything we know.
