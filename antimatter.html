The Antimatter Enigma: Mirror Opposite of the Universe

Antimatter is the exotic counterpart of regular matter, where every particle has an identical twin with opposite charge. While electrons are negative, positrons — their antimatter versions — are positive.

  • Each matter particle has an antiparticle: proton ↔ antiproton, electron ↔ positron.
  • When matter and antimatter meet, they annihilate, releasing pure energy (E = mc²).
  • Despite the Big Bang producing equal amounts of both, the universe is dominated by matter — a cosmic mystery.
  • Antimatter is real: it's used in PET scans and created in particle accelerators like CERN.
  • Scientists theorize that entire antimatter galaxies could exist in distant space.

Antimatter challenges our understanding of symmetry and physics itself. Experiments like those at the Large Hadron Collider are exploring why the observable universe is made of matter, despite quantum laws predicting perfect balance.

Harnessing antimatter for energy could be revolutionary. A single gram could release as much energy as an atomic bomb. However, storing and producing it is currently extremely expensive and unstable.

In science fiction, antimatter fuels spacecraft and powers weapons, but in real science, it's a key to unlocking the secrets of our universe’s origin.
